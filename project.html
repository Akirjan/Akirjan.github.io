Explaining gun violence in the US

In 2019, 11.1 out of every 100,000 Americans were killed by guns which was more than triple the global rate of 3. This prompts the question as to why the US is one of the worst high-income countries in controlling the negative externalities caused from firearms. This project will provide a background on the underlying statistics and costs that are directly associated to gun violence, before attempting to discover potential correlations to explain these horrific figures. Finally, this project will present the importance of gun production to the US labour market.

So to identify what the root of this continuously high death rate is in the US, I will now demonstrate several potential correlations that could explain this.

First, I leveraged the restrictive and permissive gun laws across states to establish a potential correlation with death rates in the US. In order to do this, I inspected the network’s activity of the webpage ‘ AZ-Defenders’ to find that the map I wanted to scrape was on a subpage, and after checking the source of this page, I found where the data was loaded from. I saved this data as a ‘json’ file, and this was merged with the death rate in each state in Python.

The regression clearly illustrates a positive correlation between death rates and the gun friendly index in the US. To establish the index of each state, the authors ranked the states based on various factors ranging from how lenient states are with background checks to buy firearms, to the general acceptance of guns in each state. As expected, a state with more lenient gun laws tend to have a higher death rate. An R2 of 0.50 suggest that 50 percent of the variability in the death rate is explained by the gun friendly index.

The restrictive norms and regulations would impact the gun ownership level across state. Hence, I utilised this to establish a potential relationship between death rate from firearms and the ownership of guns. I scraped data using Python from 'Wisevoter' and merged it with the death rate dataset to make the following visualisation.

This regression also demonstrates a positive relationship, but with a higher R2 of 0.55. This suggests that more of the variability in death rate is explained by the firearm ownership. Given the difference in ownership in guns, one might wonder if individuals are educated to use such a damaging weapon.

To showcase this, I used Python to web scrape data on whether having a population with more educated people affects the death rate from firearms. Hence, I regressed percentage of people that have a bachelor’s degree or higher against death rate, and also introduced a third variable of comparison in average income per state.

There is a clear negative relationship between educational level and death rate, which could imply that those in more educated areas have a greater understanding of the costs and negative externalities associated with gun mortality. As expected, the higher average income states have a higher proportion of individuals with a bachelor’s degree or higher. More interestingly, another negative relationship is established with higher earning states experiencing fewer firearm mortality.

Whilst it may seem obvious that the solution to reduce gun violence would be to stop the production and sales of guns. However, the firearm and ammunition industry is important contributor to the 2021 GDP of the US which amounted to 22,996.1 billion. To illustrate the economic output from the firearm industry, I scraped a PDF from the website ‘NSSF’ using Python.

The total economic output from the arms industry amounts to 70.52 billion which contributed to 0.3% of the 2021 US GDP. Furthermore, its contribution towards the number of jobs provided and the associated wages would all be detrimental to the lives of the individuals that rely on this as their main source of income. Finally, whilst it provides employability to the citizens, it is also paying the government via federal taxes and all these factors would be lost if the industry ceases to exist.
